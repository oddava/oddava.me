There's something odd about the way we use the internet now. Most of what we do online happens on someone else's platform — their feed, their algorithm, their rules about what counts as a post. We rent space on these services and call it presence.

A personal site is different. It's not optimized for engagement. It doesn't need to go viral. It doesn't have a content strategy. It's just a place — *your* place — where the organizing principle is whatever you care about.

## Why bother?

The honest answer is: I'm not sure it matters in any practical sense. Nobody needs a personal website. You can have a career, a social life, an entire online identity without one.

But I think there's value in building something that doesn't need to justify its existence. A personal site is one of the few things left on the internet that can just *be* — without a business model, without metrics, without trying to capture anyone's attention.

It's like having a notebook. You don't need a notebook. Your phone has a notes app. But the notebook is yours in a way that the app isn't, and that matters, even if you can't explain exactly why.

## The shape of it

I wanted this site to feel quiet. Not minimal-for-the-sake-of-minimal, but genuinely calm — the kind of place where the absence of noise is the point. No hero banners. No animations. No calls to action. Just text on a page, and enough whitespace to let it breathe.

The typography does most of the work. A good serif font at a comfortable size, with generous line height, is all you really need to make text pleasant to read. Everything else is just getting out of the way.

## What goes here

Mostly writing. Sometimes about technical things, sometimes about the experience of making things, sometimes about whatever's been on my mind. I'm not trying to build an audience or establish thought leadership. I'm just trying to think clearly, and writing is how I do that.

> "I write because I don't know what I think until I read what I say."
> — Flannery O'Connor

If something I write here is useful to you, that's great. If not, that's fine too. This space doesn't need to earn its keep.

## The tools

This site is built with [Astro](https://astro.build/), which is a static site generator that ships no JavaScript by default. Posts are written in MDX — basically Markdown with the option to embed components if I ever need to.

The whole thing is deliberately simple. No database, no CMS, no build pipeline beyond what Astro provides. I can write a post in my text editor, run a build command, and deploy. That's it.

I think there's something important about keeping your tools simple enough that you actually understand them. The moment your personal site becomes a project you need to *maintain*, it stops being fun, and you stop writing.

## An invitation

If you've been thinking about making your own site — some little corner of the internet that's just yours — I'd encourage you to do it. It doesn't have to be polished. It doesn't have to be original. It just has to be *yours*.

The internet is better when it's made of lots of small, weird, personal things, rather than a few enormous, optimized, corporate ones. Your corner doesn't have to be important. It just has to exist.
